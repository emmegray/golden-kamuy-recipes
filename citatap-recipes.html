Citatap


How to make it:

  1. Salmon head, milt, and remains from filleting are placed on a cutting board-like round piece of wood sliced from a tree trunk (itatani). The meat is chopped and beaten with a sharp heavy object, such as a hatchet.
  2. Strain bonito out, season with salt, and then add in white parts of the leeks, sliced on an angle and washed.
  3. The resulting paste is flavored with minced spring onion, pukusa, or long-stamen chive.
  4. Finally, the citatap is seasoned with dried kelp and salt.
Other than salmon, a variety of meats and fish are used to add flavor to citatap such as Japanese dace, bathyraja lindbergi, masu salmon, Japanese fluvial sculpin, deer, bear, tanuki, rabbit, and chipmunk. Citatap was well suited to the meat of older animals, as it made the tough meat easier to eat.[7]: 108–114  Citatap was primarily made in the winter, when food spoiled more slowly, and was eaten over a number of days. If the citatap was not particularly fresh, it was formed into balls and added to soup
